\section{Introduction to Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS)}
The development of Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS) is necessary due to the expansion of cities worldwide. These systems investigate the traffic environment, particularly when congestion and accidents pose a risk to public safety. Traditionally, traffic is monitored using inductive loop detectors, sensors, and manual observation. These methods have significant drawbacks such as sensors that do not provide spatial context, fatigue-related mistakes and the inability to detect lateral interactions which lead to slower response times to incidents \cite{robles-serrano_automatic_2021}. To counteract these issues, deep learning and computer vision-based systems which utilise automated video analysis to provide a non-intrusive approach, have leveraged existing Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV) networks to gather detailed, high-dimensional vehicle paths, types, and interactions. The emergence of these systems has been facilitated by the advancement of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for object detection, alongside datasets such as the Car Accident Detection and Prediction (CADP) benchmark which acts as an essential resource for training and testing prediction models. Expanding on this, the primary capability of any vision-driven ITS starts with the identification and tracking of road users. This task requires a strict equilibrium between inference speed and accuracy, as models related to road and public safety must be both fast and accurate.

\section{Traffic Anomaly Detection Frameworks}
Traffic anomaly detection primarily involves identifying events such as accidents and atypical movement patterns that deviate from standard traffic behaviour, this necessitates the capacity to distinguish between minor irregularities, such as vehicles momentarily stopping, and significant safety concerns.

A notable instance of this methodology was proposed by Aboah et al. \cite{aboah_vision-based_2021}, who developed a hybrid system that combines YOLOv5 for spatial vehicle detection with decision tree logic for anomaly classification. Their findings demonstrate that the integration of deep learning techniques with rule-based reasoning enhances interpretability by explicitly segregating visual perception from logical inference.

However, such sequential architectures are inherently susceptible to the accumulation of errors. In these hybrid systems, the logical layer is entirely contingent upon the flawless performance of the perceptual component. Consequently, if the spatial detector fails to identify a severely deformed, fragmented, or occluded vehicle amidst the chaos of a physical collision, the subsequent rule-based logic is not activated, rendering the system effectively blind to the event. To mitigate this vulnerability and prevent the omission of critical alerts, recent scholarly literature increasingly advocates for end-to-end spatiotemporal frameworks that do not depend on fragile spatial bounding boxes.

An alternative approach to spatial-temporal analysis has been introduced within the Intelligent Intersection framework, utilising a dual-stream Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) \cite{huang_intelligent_2019}. This architecture combines parallel appearance and motion streams to simultaneously assimilate static visual features alongside dynamic kinematic information. Such a fusion enhances the system’s ability to detect complex vehicle interactions and imminent collision risks with greater efficacy. By analysing raw pixel shifts continuously rather than relying solely on discrete spatial tracking parameters, the CNN can identify early physical indicators of accidents, such as vehicle skidding (atypical motion), prior to the occurrence of a structural impact (atypical appearance). This methodology effectively highlights the importance of moving beyond fragile, rule-based tracking logic, instead enabling deep neural networks to directly interpret the unstructured kinetic energy associated with an incident.

\section{Video Anomaly Detection (VAD) Paradigms}
Video Anomaly Detection (VAD) is a vital area within computer vision focused on identifying spatial or temporal deviations from typical behavioural patterns in surveillance footage. As municipal camera networks increase in scale, the volume of video data surpasses the capacity for manual monitoring, thereby highlighting the need for automated VAD systems \cite{hasan_learning_2016}.

In recent years, VAD approaches have primarily developed along two main architectural pathways: reconstruction-based anomaly detection and classification-based anomaly detection.

Reconstruction-based VAD approaches traditionally utilise a semi-supervised learning paradigm, predicated on the assumption that anomalies are inherently rare and highly variable. Given the computational impracticality of training an algorithm on every conceivable type of incident or unlawful activity, reconstruction models are typically trained solely on ‘normal’ video data \cite{gong_memorizing_2019}. Frameworks employing Autoencoders (AE) or Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) learn the latent representations of consistent traffic or pedestrian movement patterns. During inference, the model endeavours to reconstruct the incoming video frame based on its learned parameters. When the scene reflects normal activity, the reconstruction error remains low. Conversely, if a sudden collision or unusual event occurs it is unable to accurately predict or reconstruct the frame, resulting in a high reconstruction error. This discrepancy is subsequently flagged as an anomaly \cite{liu_future_2018}. While these methods are highly adaptable to new environments, they often face challenges with elevated false-positive rates, as benign variations such as changes in lighting or weather conditions can also induce large reconstruction errors.

Conversely, classification-based VAD frameworks, including purely spatiotemporal architectures, typically employ supervised or fully discriminative approaches. Instead of relying on the absence of normal patterns, these models are trained to explicitly recognise the physical and visual signatures of specific anomalies, such as sudden kinetic deformations in vehicle collisions \cite{sultani_real-world_2018}. Classification models commonly utilise deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract spatial features, alongside Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or dense optical flow techniques to capture temporal dynamics.

By training directly on annotated datasets that include both normal and anomalous events, classification-based methods generally demonstrate higher precision and increased robustness against environmental noise compared to reconstruction-based techniques. Therefore, in safety-critical applications such as traffic collision detection, where the deterministic identification of significant kinetic events is essential, discriminative classification paradigms are predominantly preferred in contemporary literature.

\section{The Role of Motion Representation in Artificial Intelligence}
In the field of computer vision, understanding complex events necessitates algorithms that go beyond processing static spatial features; they must inherently grasp temporal dynamics. The pivotal significance of motion representation within artificial intelligence was formally established by Simonyan and Zisserman \cite{simonyan_two-stream_2014}, who introduced the two-stream convolutional architecture for video analysis. Their groundbreaking research demonstrated that explicitly separating spatial appearance (standard RGB frames) from temporal motion (optical flow) enabled neural networks to attain markedly higher accuracy. They established that networks trained solely on motion fields could interpret the physical kinetics of actions without being influenced by the static visual appearance of the objects involved \cite{simonyan_two-stream_2014}.

Optical flow characterises this motion by mathematically quantifying the apparent pixel-level displacement between successive video frames. Dense optical flow methods, such as the quadratic polynomial expansion algorithm devised by Farnebäck \cite{goos_two-frame_2003}, determine a distinct motion vector for each individual pixel throughout the entire spatial domain. This process yields a dense, spatiotemporal matrix that encapsulates both the magnitude and direction of local kinetic energy.

In the context of traffic anomaly detection, reliance solely on this motion representation offers significant advantages. The visual characteristics of a vehicle are fundamentally irrelevant to the mechanics of a collision. Rather, accidents are characterised by abrupt, chaotic changes in velocity and trajectory. By converting raw surveillance footage into dense optical flow heatmaps, the artificial intelligence is compelled to disregard static background noise, structural occlusions, and complex textures \cite{nawaratne_spatiotemporal_2020}. The model evaluates exclusively the unstructured kinetic energy within the scene. This isolation of motion signatures enables deep learning classifiers to identify the genuine physical indicators of a collision thus rendering the framework highly resilient to environmental degradation and variations in illumination that often undermine conventional spatial object detectors.

\section{Spatiotemporal Analysis via Dense Optical Flow}
Traditional object detection methods, whilst highly effective at isolating individual vehicle trajectories through bounding boxes, frequently encounter difficulties in interpreting the messy spatial dynamics and overall severity of multi-vehicle collisions \cite{robles-serrano_automatic_2021, aboah_vision-based_2021, huang_intelligent_2019}. When vehicles collide, their visual representations tend to blend or break apart, making it complex to track them consistently. Recent scholarly work, such as that by Singh et al. \cite{singh_surveillance_2025}, emphasises the importance of capturing unstructured spatiotemporal dependencies to enable accurate detection of road accidents. Although the authors proposed the utilisation of complex transformer-based architectures to understand these temporal relationships, these approaches end up being heavy on the computing side, which may be prohibitive for real-time deployment at edge devices within municipal surveillance contexts.

To quantify unstructured kinetic energy without the delay that comes with transformer models, numerous vision-based frameworks depend on dense optical flow techniques \cite{goos_two-frame_2003, huang_intelligent_2019}. Unlike sparse methods, such as the Lucas-Kanade algorithm, which track only a limited set of high-contrast corner points, the dense optical flow algorithm developed by Gunnar Farnebäck \cite{goos_two-frame_2003} calculates a unique movement vector for every single pixel in the frame.

The mathematical basis of the Farnebäck algorithm is based on quadratic polynomial expansion. Basically, it estimates the local area around each pixel in consecutive frames using quadratic polynomials. For a specified 2D spatial coordinate vector, denoted as $\mathbf{x}$, the intensity within the initial frame is approximated as:

\begin{equation}
f_1(\mathbf{x}) \approx \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{A}_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1^T \mathbf{x} + c_1
\label{eq:farneback_poly1}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{x}$ represents the 2D local spatial coordinate vector defined as $(x, y)^T$.
    \item $T$ denotes the standard matrix transpose operation.
    \item $\mathbf{A}_1$ is a symmetric matrix representing the second-order spatial derivatives, capturing the quadratic surface or curvature of the image intensity.
    \item $\mathbf{b}_1$ is a vector capturing the first-order derivatives, representing the linear gradient of the image intensity.
    \item $c_1$ is a scalar representing the constant term, corresponding to the absolute pixel intensity value at the centre of the local neighbourhood.
\end{itemize}

Assuming that the second frame is a global translation $\mathbf{d}$ of the first frame, the polynomial for the second frame expands to:

\begin{equation}
f_2(\mathbf{x}) = f_1(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{d}) = (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{d})^T \mathbf{A}_1 (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{d}) + \mathbf{b}_1^T (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{d}) + c_1
\label{eq:farneback_poly2}
\end{equation}

By equating the coefficients of this transformed polynomial with the empirically observed coefficients in the second frame the algorithm straight away gives the precise displacement field $\mathbf{d}$ \cite{goos_two-frame_2003}. When it comes to traffic monitoring, pulling out this raw movement signature lets tracking systems display the exact spot, direction, and severity of a collision as a space-time heatmap, no matter what type of vehicle it is or how good the lighting conditions are.

\section{The History of Resnet-18}
The evolution of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in the early 2010s was marked by a persistent endeavour to develop deeper architectures. Following the success of AlexNet in 2012, researchers posited that increasing the number of convolutional layers would proportionally enhance the network's capacity to extract complex, high-level semantic features from images.

However, as networks grew to exceed twenty layers, the issue of degradation emerged. Contrary to concerns of overfitting, the training accuracy of these deeper networks plateaued and subsequently deteriorated rapidly \cite{he_deep_2016}. This phenomenon was attributed to the vanishing or exploding of gradients during backpropagation, which hindered the early layers of the network from effectively updating their weights \cite{krizhevsky_imagenet_2017}.

To address this fundamental architectural bottleneck, Kaiming He and his team at Microsoft Research introduced the Deep Residual Network (ResNet) framework in 2015 \cite{he_deep_2016}. The primary innovation of the ResNet architecture lies in the concept of the "residual block," which integrates shortcut connections (or skip connections). Rather than compelling the stacked nonlinear layers to directly approximate a desired underlying mapping, these skip connections bypass one or more layers, executing an identity mapping. Consequently, the network is trained to learn a residual function. This development enabled gradients to propagate unimpeded backwards through the network, facilitating the successful training of architectures comprising 50, 101, and even 152 layers \cite{he_deep_2016}.

Within the ResNet family, ResNet-18 has established itself as the most lightweight and foundational variant. Comprising eighteen deep layers, it was designed to provide the advantages of residual learning whilst minimising computational and memory demands compared to its deeper counterparts \cite{targ_resnet_2016}. Due to its elegant compromise between parameter efficiency and high classification accuracy, ResNet-18 has swiftly become a standard benchmark within the computer vision community. In contemporary applications, notably in edge computing and real-time surveillance scenarios such as traffic anomaly detection, ResNet-18 remains highly pertinent. Its compact architecture enables rapid inference on constrained hardware, whilst its residual framework ensures the retention of robust feature-extraction capabilities necessary to interpret complex spatial environments \cite{han_deep_2015}.

\section{Deep Learning Classification using ResNet Architectures}
Following the extraction of kinetic features, the classification of anomaly severity necessitates the deployment of robust deep learning architectures \cite{robles-serrano_automatic_2021, aboah_vision-based_2021, basheer_ahmed_real-time_2023}. In the past, as CNNs got deeper to catch more complex details, they ran into the vanishing gradient problem. During the backward propagation of errors in training, the gradients diminished to negligible levels, resulting in the saturation and rapid deterioration of the network’s accuracy.

To get around this architectural limitation, He and his team \cite{he_deep_2016} came up with the Deep Residual Network (ResNet) framework. The central innovation of ResNet lies in the residual block, which incorporates a "shortcut" or "skip" connection that bypasses one or more layers. Instead of making the stacked layers learn a complex, unreferenced mapping $\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{x})$, the network is trained to learn a residual mapping $\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{H}(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbf{x}$ \cite{shah_cadp_2018}. The final output of the block thus becomes an additive identity mapping:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y} = \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x}, \{W_i\}) + \mathbf{x}
\label{eq:resnet}
\end{equation}

This structure guarantees that gradients can propagate backwards through the network without obstruction, thereby facilitating substantially deeper architectures without compromising generalisation.

Within the field of Intelligent Transport Systems, ResNet architectures have demonstrated significant efficacy in interpreting traffic anomalies \cite{basheer_ahmed_real-time_2023, oluwadamilola_traffic_2026}. Recent work by Oluwadamilola and Emmanuel \cite{oluwadamilola_traffic_2026} highlighted how well ResNet-18 does when it comes to classifying how serious traffic accidents are. Their results suggest that ResNet-18 offers a preferable balance between classification accuracy and computational efficiency when compared to deeper, parameter-intensive architectures such as ResNet-50 or VGG-16. That makes it an ideal choice for quick, real-time use at the edge.

Furthermore, to address the inherent class imbalances commonly encountered in traffic datasets, where normal driving cases totally outnumber accidents \cite{lin_focal_2017, shah_cadp_2018}. Classifiers such as ResNet are frequently combined with the Focal Loss function \cite{lin_focal_2017, oluwadamilola_traffic_2026}. Unlike standard Cross-Entropy loss, Focal Loss tweaks the penalty on the fly, depending on how confident the model is about its predictions:

\begin{equation}
FL(p_t) = -\alpha_t(1-p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
\label{eq:focal_loss}
\end{equation}

This formulation basically gives less importance to the loss from easy-to-classify examples (like normal, steady traffic) and directs the network’s optimisation exclusively towards challenging, misclassified instances, such as differentiating between heavy braking and a low-speed collision.

\section{Overcoming Class Imbalance in Rare-Event Detection}
In the field of traffic surveillance, anomaly detection fundamentally constitutes a problem of rare-event classification. Video datasets are inherently imbalanced, with routine driving behaviours comprising most of the recorded footage, whilst severe anomalies represent only a marginal fraction of the data \cite{sultani_real-world_2018}. This pronounced class imbalance presents a significant challenge for training deep neural networks. When subjected to such disproportionate distributions, conventional optimisation metrics, such as binary Cross-Entropy loss, tend to be dominated by the easily recognised majority class. Consequently, the network often converges to a suboptimal local minimum, wherein it predominantly predicts the majority class (normal traffic) to attain a superficially high overall accuracy, whilst entirely failing to identify the critical minority class (incidents) \cite{buda_systematic_2018}.

To address this dataset bias, conventional machine learning approaches often utilise data-level interventions, such as the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) or random undersampling. However, in the context of high-dimensional spatiotemporal data, such as dense optical flow matrices, these methods are frequently computationally burdensome and may introduce synthetic noise that compromises the genuine kinetic physics of an impact \cite{haibo_he_learning_2009}.

Consequently, contemporary spatiotemporal frameworks increasingly depend on interventions at the algorithmic level, notably cost-sensitive learning functions. A significant advancement in this field is the Focal Loss function, introduced by Lin et al. \cite{lin_focal_2017}. Focal Loss mathematically alters the conventional Cross-Entropy formulation by incorporating a modulating factor that adaptively scales the gradient according to the model’s confidence in its predictions. This approach effectively diminishes the loss contribution from easily classified examples and imposes a disproportionately higher penalty for misclassifications of more challenging, infrequent instances \cite{lin_focal_2017}. By compelling the optimiser to focus its learning efforts solely on the minority class characterised by anomalies, deep residual networks can attain heightened sensitivity in anomaly detection without the necessity for manual dataset balancing or synthetic data augmentation.

\section{Benchmark Datasets: The Role of CADP}
The efficacy of these advanced models is highly contingent upon the availability of robust benchmark datasets, with the Car Accident Detection and Prediction (CADP) dataset serving as a principal resource for the assessment of surveillance-based methodologies. CADP was created to address the scarcity of publicly accessible datasets containing precise spatio-temporal annotations pertinent to accident prediction \cite{shah_cadp_2018}. Comprising 1,416 video clips obtained from YouTube CCTV viewpoints, CADP presents standardised Time-to-Accident (TTA) annotations. The TTA metric is formally characterised as the temporal interval between an algorithm’s initial prediction of an anomaly and the precise moment of physical impact. These specific annotations, which on average precede a collision by 3.69 seconds, are fundamental to the development and evaluation of predictive early warning systems. This consistent temporal framework allows researchers to assess not only detection accuracy but also prediction latency, a factor of critical importance for collision avoidance. 

The third-person perspective embodied in CADP captures the comprehensive interactions among various traffic participants \cite{shah_cadp_2018}, rendering it markedly more advantageous than dashcam-centric datasets such as the Dashcam Accident Dataset (DAD) or AnAn Accident Detection (A3D) for monitoring urban infrastructure within smart cities. Whilst DAD and A3D furnish valuable ego-centric data relevant to autonomous vehicles, they often lack the broader spatial context provided by the stationary surveillance perspectives characteristic of CADP, which is essential for analysing flow dynamics across multiple lanes.

\section{Model Ensembling for Enhanced Reliability}
To make these detections more reliable and mitigate the frequent false-positive rates observed in single-model systems, researchers are increasingly adopting model ensembling techniques. Ensembling combines predictions from different models to enhance overall accuracy and reduce false positives in surveillance footage \cite{jokhio_scalable_2024}. Common ensemble methods, such as voting and stacking, are widely employed. In a voting ensemble, multiple classifiers (e.g., Random Forest, Support Vector Machines, CNNs) evaluate the same input data, with the final decision determined by a majority vote to reduce the bias inherent in individual models.

Stacking offers a more advanced approach by training a meta-learner (typically a simple neural network or logistic regression) to synthesise the outputs of base models \cite{liu_hybrid_2025}. This technique often surpasses the performance of standalone models in assessing accident severity by learning which predictors are most significant in various situations. The I3D-ConvLSTM2D architecture exemplifies a more sophisticated multi-stream ensemble that combines RGB frames with optical flow information to identify both spatial features and motion irregularities over time \cite{adewopo_smart_2023}. This dual approach allows the model to fuse local object characteristics with the overall scene context, significantly improving Area Under the Curve (AUC) scores on standard datasets. Furthermore, recent studies have introduced "Expert Ensembles" (Xen), comprising specialised sub-models that monitor global flow, object interactions, and individual behaviour \cite{orlova_simplifying_2025}. These scores are subsequently combined using a Kalman filter to produce a stable, noise-resistant anomaly signal.

\section{Explainable AI and Edge Computing Integration}
Beyond these methodological and data-driven advancements, the integration of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) approaches addresses the 'black box' issue associated with deploying pure deep learning models within safety-critical systems. Techniques such as Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) are particularly valuable within spatiotemporal frameworks. By applying Grad-CAM directly to dense optical flow heatmaps, systems can visually emphasise the specific pixels that prompted the accident warning. This facilitates human operators in verifying that the neural network is genuinely attending to the physical mechanics of a collision rather than arbitrary background noise.

While traditional hybrid systems offered inherent transparency through explicitly stating violated parameters (for example, rule-based speed thresholds), the shift towards more robust, purely spatiotemporal neural networks renders XAI essential. It ensures compliance with regulatory standards and enhances public confidence by transforming a complex, opaque classification system into a transparent decision-support tool \cite{pandey_explainable_2026}.

Furthermore, to satisfy the stringent latency requirements of real-time municipal surveillance, researchers are progressively adopting edge computing architectures. Instead of depending on computationally intensive multi-stream ensemble methods or transmitting large volumes of raw video data to centralised cloud servers, contemporary frameworks emphasise localised processing. By deploying highly optimised kinematic extraction algorithms alongside compact, parameter-efficient classifiers, such as ResNet-18, directly onto or near camera hardware, systems can markedly minimise transmission delays. This architectural realignment enables the sub-second response times essential for prompt anomaly detection and emergency response coordination \cite{somireddypalli_maneesh_kumar_ensemble_2025}. To facilitate the successful deployment of these systems, researchers frequently utilise model compression techniques, including weight quantisation and network pruning, which allow sophisticated spatiotemporal pipelines to function effectively on the resource-limited hardware typical of existing CCTV networks.

\section{Multi-Modal Integration and Vision-Language Models}
Beyond relying solely on visual data, the future of traffic anomaly detection is increasingly dependent upon the integration of diverse data sources, such as LiDAR and V2X (Vehicle-to-Everything) communications. While camera-based systems are cost-effective, incorporating acoustic sensors or roadside LiDAR can significantly enhance accuracy, particularly in challenging weather conditions, such as heavy rain or snow, where optical sensors frequently encounter difficulties. Leveraging the geometric details of objects in video sequences can mitigate inherent visual biases in current datasets, thereby yielding models with improved generalisation capabilities \cite{mahmood_new_2023}.

Furthermore, the emergence of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) offers a novel approach to traffic anomaly detection, substantially improving reliability within complex environments by enabling the semantic interpretation of traffic incidents. These models can describe an incident not merely as a set of coordinates, but as a coherent narrative (for example, "a white saloon failed to stop and collided with a pedestrian"), thereby streamlining communication with emergency services. Achieving this level of real-time comprehension requires advanced architectures that balance rapid reasoning with deep predictive capabilities, thereby expanding the overarching potential of automated traffic monitoring \cite{song_real-time_2025}.

\section{Conclusion}
In summary, prior research and the assessment of contemporary surveillance frameworks suggest that, whilst individual components of spatial traffic monitoring have attained significant advancements under steady-state conditions, their inherent vulnerability during multi-vehicle collisions reveals substantial operational limitations. By abandoning these fragile spatial trackers in favour of purely spatiotemporal analysis, researchers can develop frameworks capable of directly understanding the unstructured kinetic energy generated during an incident.

The integration of dense kinetic mapping techniques, such as Farnebäck optical flow, with the robust pattern recognition capabilities of deep residual networks like ResNet-18, provides a highly resilient, calibration-free approach to traffic anomaly detection. The CADP dataset constitutes a crucial resource in advancing this paradigm, offering the essential temporal annotations necessary to assess the effectiveness of deep learning models in capturing sudden, chaotic fluctuations in physical momentum without dependence on explicit vehicle categorisation.

As the discipline progresses towards improved interpretability and the integration of edge computing, the transition from sequential, rule-based logic to purely spatiotemporal neural networks will remain central to the development of safer and more efficient Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS). This purely algorithmic methodology not only overcomes the geometric limitations and manual calibration requirements associated with traditional CCTV surveillance but also enables sub-second anomaly detection, which is vital for proactive traffic management. Ultimately, the realisation of such kinetic-aware frameworks will significantly contribute to the development of more resilient urban infrastructures, facilitate emergency response operations, and enhance public safety on a broader municipal scale.
